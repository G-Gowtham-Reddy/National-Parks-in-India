About National Parks of India

Conserving Nature for Future Generations

India is home to over 100 national parks, each playing a vital role in conserving biodiversity and providing habitats for countless species.

These parks are vital to maintaining ecological balance and ensuring the survival of endangered species like the Bengal tiger, Indian elephant, and one-horned rhinoceros.

Jim Corbett National Park

1. History and Background
• Named after the hunter-turned-conservationist Jim Corbett, this park was originally called Hailey National Park. It was established in 1936 and later renamed to honor Corbett, who advocated for wildlife conservation.
• Known for its role in tiger conservation, Jim Corbett was one of the first parks to join Project Tiger in 1973, a flagship conservation project aimed at protecting Bengal tigers.
2. Zones and Safari Experiences
• The park is divided into six tourism zones: Dhikala, Bijrani, Jhirna, Dhela, Durga Devi, and Sonanadi.
• Dhikala is popular for its overnight stays and scenic landscapes, while Bijrani is known for frequent tiger sightings.
• Safari types: Jeep safari, Canter safari (large open vehicles for group safaris), and elephant safaris are available in specific zones.
3. Flora and Fauna
• Vegetation: Sal trees, riverine belts, and grasslands. The diversity in plant life supports a wide range of species.
• Other Wildlife: Leopards, wild boars, sloth bears, and over 600 bird species, making it a haven for bird watchers.

Kaziranga National Park


1. Unique Biodiversity and Ecosystem
• Kaziranga’s diverse landscape of floodplains and grasslands creates the perfect habitat for the one-horned rhinoceros, a symbol of Assam.
• The park has one of the highest densities of tigers among protected areas, though they are difficult to spot due to the tall grasslands.
2. Zones and Safari Details
• The park is divided into four main zones for tourism: Central (Kohora), Western (Bagori), Eastern (Agaratoli), and Burapahar.
• Safari options include jeep and elephant safaris, with elephant safaris being popular for close encounters with rhinos and deer.
• Best time for safaris: Early morning and late afternoon, as animals are more active.
3. Challenges and Conservation
• Seasonal Flooding: Annual floods from the Brahmaputra River can cause significant habitat disturbance. The flooding, however, also rejuvenates the grasslands.
• Anti-Poaching Efforts: Poaching is a threat, primarily for rhino horn trafficking, but strict measures have helped reduce these incidents.

Ranthambore National Park


1. Historical Significance
• Ranthambore Fort, dating back to the 10th century, is located within the park, offering a unique blend of history and wildlife.
• The fort’s strategic position offers panoramic views of the park, and its ancient temples, mosques, and step-wells provide a historical context to the landscape.
2. Tiger Tourism and Conservation
• Ranthambore is renowned for tiger tourism, and it’s common to see tigers near water bodies like Padam Talao and Rajbagh Lake.
• The park is divided into 10 zones; Zones 1-5 are the best for tiger sightings, though sightings are possible throughout the park.
3. Wildlife Beyond Tigers
• Other Species: Leopards, sloth bears, Indian gazelles, and a variety of birds like Indian owlets and serpent eagles.
• Flora: Dry deciduous forests with Dhok trees, interspersed with lakes and small ponds, making it a semi-arid yet rich ecosystem.

Sundarbans National Park


1. Unique Mangrove Ecosystem
• The Sundarbans, as the world's largest mangrove forest, plays a crucial role in flood control and serves as a protective barrier against cyclones and storms in the region.
• It's the only place in India where the unique behavior of “swimming tigers” is observed, with Bengal tigers adapted to a semi-aquatic lifestyle.
2. Biodiversity
• Home to endangered species like the estuarine crocodile, Olive Ridley turtles, and the Gangetic dolphin.
• Rich birdlife includes kingfishers, eagles, and migratory birds that flock to the delta during winter.
3. Boat Safari and Visitor Experience
• Due to the marshy terrain, visitors explore Sundarbans via boat safaris rather than traditional land safaris.
• Boat safaris provide a glimpse of the mangrove landscape and a chance to see wildlife near water sources. Permits are required, and organized tours are common.
4. Conservation Efforts and Challenges
• Ongoing conservation is crucial due to threats from rising sea levels and human-wildlife conflicts.
• The park plays a key role in tiger conservation under Project Tiger.

Gir National Park


1. Habitat and Terrain
• Gir has a unique dry deciduous forest ecosystem with an abundance of teak, jamun, and flame-of-the-forest trees.
• Terrain: A mix of open grasslands, rocky hills, and water bodies, which supports a diverse range of flora and fauna.
2. Asiatic Lions
• Gir is the last refuge for Asiatic lions, with a population that has been gradually increasing due to successful conservation.
• To reduce genetic isolation, efforts are underway to establish a second Asiatic lion population in another Indian sanctuary.
3. Other Wildlife and Flora
• Apart from lions, Gir is home to leopards, marsh crocodiles, hyenas, sambar deer, and a variety of birds.
• The park has around 400 plant species, contributing to its ecosystem's biodiversity.
4. Safari Options
• Gir offers jeep safaris, which operate in three designated zones for tourism. Permits are limited to maintain the ecosystem and avoid disturbances to wildlife.
• Best visiting time: December to March, as the hot summer season limits animal activity.
5. Conservation Challenges
• Threats: Human-wildlife conflict, especially as lions sometimes wander into nearby villages.
• The park's management team is working on strategies to minimize these conflicts and ensure the safety of both wildlife and villagers.
